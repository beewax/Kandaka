Overview
Sudan stands at a critical economic crossroads. The 2011 independence of South Sudan stripped the country of approximately three-quarters of its oil production, causing crude oil export revenues to plummet from nearly $11 billion in 2010 to just under $1.8 billion in 2012. This fiscal shock forced a national rethink — and solar energy has emerged as one of the most promising paths forward.
This research examines Sudan's solar energy potential across five dimensions: natural resources, technology, economics, policy, and public awareness. The findings are significant for anyone tracking economic development in the region.
Why Sudan Is Uniquely Positioned for Solar
Sudan receives between 8.5 and 11 hours of sunshine per day, and its solar energy density ranges from 436 to 639 W/m² — well above the global average of 100–250 W/m². The country's northern and western regions are particularly rich in solar irradiance, placing Sudan among the best-positioned countries in the world for solar exploitation.
Unlike hydropower — Sudan's current dominant energy source at roughly 70% of total power supply — solar energy requires no cross-border agreements with neighboring Nile riparian states, no massive dam infrastructure, and no lengthy multi-decade lead times. It is also modular: a solar solution can power a single clinic, a farming village, or a national grid.
The Energy Gap: A Development Crisis
Sudan's energy deficit is stark and economically damaging:

Only 30% of the population has access to electricity, mostly in urban areas
Over 70% of the population lives in rural areas with little to no power access
Biomass (firewood and charcoal) accounts for roughly 57% of total energy consumption, causing serious environmental and public health harm
The agriculture sector — which contributes about 41% of GDP and employs over 65% of the labor force — is severely constrained by unreliable energy access

Energy poverty is not just a humanitarian issue; it is a direct brake on productivity, trade, and GDP growth.
Solar as an Economic Driver
The research identifies solar energy as a multi-dimensional economic opportunity:
For rural communities: Stand-alone photovoltaic systems can power homes, schools, clinics, and mosques without connecting to the national grid — critical for remote areas where grid extension is prohibitively expensive.
For agriculture: Solar water pumping supports both drinking water access and irrigation. Solar dryers extend the shelf life of crops and enable value-added export products. A Uganda case study cited in the research showed how solar dryers helped women's groups establish a dried-fruit export company that reached European markets within three years.
For job creation: IRENA estimates the renewable energy sector created around 7.7 million direct and indirect jobs globally in 2014, with solar PV as the largest single employer at 2.5 million jobs worldwide — a model Sudan could begin to replicate domestically.
For food security: PV-powered vaccine refrigerators are proving critical in areas where grid power is unavailable, supporting health infrastructure that underpins workforce productivity.
The Policy Gap: Where Opportunity Is Being Lost
Despite Sudan's natural solar endowment, actual solar deployment remains minimal — photovoltaic systems provide only 0.03% of Sudan's electricity supply. The barriers are primarily policy-driven, not resource-driven:

Punitive import duties on solar PV equipment make the technology less cost-competitive than diesel generators
Fuel subsidies artificially lower the cost of conventional energy, skewing the market against renewables
No feed-in tariff or comparable mechanism to encourage private solar investment
Limited access to soft loans or concessional financing for solar projects
Weak grid infrastructure makes large-scale grid-connected solar projects difficult

A 2006 comparison study in Northern Kurdufan and Dongola found that Sudan's customs and tax policies added enough cost to PV systems to make diesel generators the more economical choice for rural communities — a policy outcome directly counter to development goals.
Sudan's Stated Ambitions vs. Ground Reality
Sudan's Intended Nationally Determined Contributions report commits to:

1,000 MW of solar PV (on- and off-grid)
100 MW of Concentrated Solar Power
Installation of 1.1 million Solar Home Systems by 2030
Total energy sector investment of approximately $7.55 billion

The gap between stated ambition and on-the-ground action is the central challenge. The Ministry of Water Resources and Electricity has acknowledged the core obstacles plainly: financing gaps, lack of supportive policies such as feed-in tariffs and power purchase agreements, and weak grid infrastructure.

Recommended Path Forward

Prioritize off-grid stand-alone PV systems for rural areas where grid extension is not feasible
Reform tax and import duty structures on solar equipment to make PV economically competitive with diesel
Introduce financing mechanisms including government subsidies, soft bank loans, and micro-finance through NGOs
Establish public-private partnerships to share investment risk and attract domestic and foreign capital
Develop a regulatory framework including feed-in tariffs and power purchase agreements
Launch public awareness campaigns targeting both end users and local financial institutions
Leverage strategic partnerships with Gulf Cooperation Council states to secure financing for larger grid-connected solar infrastructure

Bottom Line
Sudan possesses one of the most favorable solar resource bases in the world. Its largest economic sector — agriculture — is energy-starved. More than 70% of its population lacks electricity access. The technology is available, increasingly affordable, and proven across the region.
The binding constraint is not sunlight — it is policy will, financing architecture, and institutional capacity. For a country seeking to rebuild its economic foundations, solar energy represents one of the most concrete, scalable, and immediately impactful levers available.
