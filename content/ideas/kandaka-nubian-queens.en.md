The word Kandaka appears in the New Testament. A treasury official travelling from the court of the "Kandake, queen of the Ethiopians" -- the Greek word for any land south of Egypt -- is described returning from Jerusalem in the Acts of the Apostles. The Roman geographer Strabo wrote about the one-eyed Kandake who led her armies against the legions of Augustus Caesar. Pliny the Elder mentioned the Kandakai in his encyclopaedia. These women were not legendary figures or mythological queens. They were heads of state, military commanders, and economic administrators of one of the most sophisticated civilisations in the ancient world -- a civilisation located, for most of its history, in what is now Sudan.

The site of this article takes its name from them. That choice is not decorative. The Kandakat governed a state that for nearly a thousand years managed complex trade networks, built an industrial iron economy, developed an original writing system, and held its own against Egypt, Rome, and Aksum in succession. Understanding what they built -- and why it matters for Sudan today -- is the purpose of this article.

## Who the Kandakat Were

The title Kandaka (plural Kandakat) designated the queen mother or ruling queen of the Kingdom of Kush, the Nubian state that dominated the Middle Nile Valley from roughly the 8th century BCE until the 4th century CE. The institution was not symbolic. Kandakat exercised real executive, military, and religious authority. Several ruled in their own right, without a co-regent king.

Shandakhete, who ascended the Kushite throne around the 2nd century BCE when the polity was concentrated in Meroe, was regarded as one of the earliest ruling African queens and one of the earliest female rulers in the world. She ruled without a pharaoh and held complete power over the Meroitic Empire.

The most documented Kandaka is Amanirenas, who reigned from approximately 40 to 10 BCE. When Roman forces under Augustus seized control of Egypt from Mark Antony and Cleopatra, Amanirenas planned and then carried out her kingdom's attacks on occupying Roman forces. The war lasted three years. Queen Amanirenas is one of the most famous Meroitic queens because of her role in leading the Kushite army against the Romans in a war that lasted three years, from 25 BCE to 22 BCE. This war is largely responsible for halting Rome's southward expansion in Africa.

The peace terms that ended the war were negotiated at Samos, where Kushite envoys met with Augustus himself. The peace treaty concluded in 21 BC preserved the Kingdom of Kush's sovereignty over its core Meroitic territories, preventing any Roman administrative incorporation or permanent garrisons beyond the first cataract. Rome, at the height of its imperial power, granted concessions. The bronze head of Augustus Caesar -- recovered from beneath the steps of a victory temple at Meroe, where it had been deliberately buried as a trophy under the feet of its captors -- now sits in the British Museum. It is one of the most striking objects in their collection, and almost no one knows it came from Sudan.

Amanirenas was followed by Amanishakheto and Amanitore, each of whom continued the political and architectural legacy. Amanishakheto is known from several monuments: the Amun-temple of Kawa, a stela from Meroe, inscriptions of a palace at Wad ban Naqa, and her pyramid at Meroe. Her burial goods -- spectacular gold jewellery including armlets, rings, and shield rings of extraordinary craftsmanship -- were removed from her pyramid in 1834 by an Italian treasure hunter named Giuseppe Ferlini, who dynamited the pyramid to reach the burial chamber. The jewellery is now in Munich and Berlin. The pyramid's upper section was destroyed. This pattern -- Nubian wealth extracted, Nubian monuments damaged, Nubian civilisation relocated to European institutions -- has defined the archaeology of Sudan for two centuries.

## The Economy They Built

The standard development narrative positions Sudan as a country with abundant natural resources that has failed to translate them into prosperity. The archaeological record of the Meroitic period tells a different story: Sudan was, for nearly a millennium, a country that did exactly that -- and the mechanisms by which it did so are instructive.

**Iron production at industrial scale.** Discoveries of massive slag heaps, kilns, and forges in the outskirts of Meroe and the neighboring town of Hamadab, along with the remains of iron and copper tools and gold and bronze jewelry, attest to the city's importance as a center of local industries. The iron-slag mounds in particular earned it the nickname of the "Birmingham of Africa."

Meroe is famous for its landscape with "many black mounds" on the site. In 1911 the Egyptologist A. H. Sayce declared that Meroe must have been the "Birmingham of Africa" as indicated by the presence of these mounds. Modern metallurgical analysis has confirmed that Meroitic-period artifacts represent a metallurgical tradition distinct from those of either sub-Saharan Africa or the Mediterranean basin -- an indigenous technological tradition, not borrowed from Egypt or Rome.

Iron production was not a cottage industry. The slag heaps at Meroe are enormous. The city sustained furnaces, tuyeres, and smithing operations across centuries, producing tools and weapons that circulated across the trade networks the state controlled. This was value-added manufacturing -- the transformation of raw ore into finished goods for export. Alexander Hamilton would have recognised it immediately.

**Trade networks of continental reach.** The city of Meroe was a crossroads for trade between sub-Saharan Africa, Egypt, the Red Sea, and beyond, facilitating commerce in gold, ivory, iron, and exotic goods. Outbound trade from its capital Meroe included ebony, elephants, gold, iron, ivory and ostrich feathers. Trade routes criss-crossed the desert and extended down the Nile river to Greco-Roman Egypt, as well as through Red Sea ports to several Middle Eastern destinations including Egypt.

The Kingdom of Kush was not a passive supplier of raw materials to more sophisticated trading partners. It controlled the trade routes -- through the Nile valley, across the Bayuda desert, through the wadis connecting the Nile to the Red Sea coast. It managed the flow of goods from the African interior to the Mediterranean world, capturing the margin between production in sub-Saharan Africa and consumption in Rome and Alexandria.

**A monetary and financial system.** The kingdom minted its own currency and established sophisticated banking systems. The existence of Meroitic coinage -- adapting and then departing from Egyptian and Greek numismatic traditions -- indicates a state with sufficient commercial sophistication to standardise exchange. This was not a barter economy.

**An original writing system.** The Meroitic script, developed in the 3rd century BCE under King Arkamani I, represented the Kushite language in both hieroglyphic and cursive forms. It was the first writing system developed in sub-Saharan Africa for a sub-Saharan African language. Meroitic script is now one of the top ten oldest writing scripts in the world and one which we have yet to decipher. The fact that it remains largely undeciphered is itself significant: it means we are reading the history of this civilisation primarily through the accounts of its rivals and trading partners -- Roman, Greek, and Egyptian sources -- rather than through its own records. The Meroitic state was literate in its own language. We simply cannot yet read what it wrote.

**Agricultural infrastructure.** The Meroitic state developed the hafir -- large cisterns for rainwater collection -- and sophisticated irrigation systems that extended cultivation beyond the Nile valley into the Butana plain. This was not subsistence farming. It was organised agricultural production at a scale sufficient to sustain urban populations and export surpluses.

## The Political Economy of Kandaka Rule

The Kandakat did not govern a primitive or tribal state. Unlike Egypt's hereditary kingship, Meroe elected rulers from royal kin, a process involving military leaders and priests. This was a more complex political system than simple hereditary monarchy -- one requiring the management of multiple institutional actors with competing interests. The Kandaka who succeeded within this system was a political operator of considerable skill, not merely a symbolic figurehead.

Unlike ancient civilisations that favoured military subjugation and bloody oppression, Nubia is famous for embodying the principles of diplomacy and always striving to maintain peace. They preferred commercial exchanges and economic benefits to military campaigns and battles. This is a generalisation with exceptions -- Amanirenas was clearly not opposed to military campaigns when necessary -- but the broader pattern is real. The Kingdom of Kush survived and prospered for centuries in a neighbourhood containing Egypt, Rome, and later Aksum through a combination of strategic military capability and sophisticated commercial diplomacy. It knew when to fight and when to trade, and it maintained the institutional capacity to do both.

The parallel with Ha-Joon Chang's argument about infant industry protection is not forced. Chang's point is that productive capacity must be built deliberately, that it requires protection from more powerful competitors during the period of development, and that the state must play an active role in directing resources toward strategic sectors. The Meroitic state did exactly this: it protected its iron production, controlled its trade routes, managed its agricultural surplus, and used military force selectively to defend the economic assets that underpinned its prosperity. It was not following Washington Consensus recommendations. It was doing what every successful developer has done.

## Why the Kingdom Fell -- and Why It Matters

The Meroitic state's decline in the 3rd and 4th centuries CE resulted from a combination of factors that development economists would recognise. Deforestation to fuel its iron industry, soil exhaustion, and shifts in trade patterns weakened the kingdom. In about 350 CE, a king of the neighboring Aksumite Kingdom invaded and sacked Meroe.

The iron industry that was Meroe's industrial base also consumed its forests. The trade routes that brought its wealth shifted -- as Red Sea routes displaced overland routes, Axum gained geographic advantage. Agricultural productivity declined as soil was depleted. These are structural economic problems, not signs of civilisational failure. They are the problems that every economy, ancient and modern, faces when it exhausts a resource base without transitioning to a new one.

The subsequent history of the Nile valley -- the Nubian Christian kingdoms of Nobatia, Makuria, and Alodia that succeeded Meroe and survived into the medieval period, the Islamic sultanates that followed, the Funj Sultanate of Sennar, the Ottoman and Egyptian occupation, British colonialism, and finally the succession of post-independence governments in Khartoum -- is a history of disruption, extraction, and governance failure layered over a civilisational substrate of far greater depth and sophistication than the disruptions acknowledge.

Sudan is not a country with no economic history. It is a country whose economic history has been systematically erased, extracted, and relocated -- to the British Museum, to the Egyptian Museum of Berlin, to the Egyptian Museum of Munich, to the institutions of disciplines that studied Nubia as an object of curiosity rather than as a civilisation with lessons to teach.

## What This Means for How Sudan Talks About Itself

The choice of name for this platform was deliberate. Kandaka -- the title of the ruling queens of Kush -- is not merely a symbol of female strength, though it is that. It is a claim about what Sudan is and where it comes from.

Development discourse, when it reaches Sudan at all, tends to treat the country as a problem to be solved: a failed state, a humanitarian emergency, a country with potential that has never been realised. This framing is not simply incomplete. It is actively misleading, because it erases the historical record of what Sudanese civilisation actually achieved when it governed itself.

The Kandakat ran an iron industry that contemporaries compared to industrial England. They managed trade networks of continental scope. They negotiated with Rome as equals and won. They built pyramids -- more pyramids than Egypt -- in the desert north of Khartoum, where they still stand, visited by a tiny fraction of the tourists who visit Giza. They developed the first writing system in sub-Saharan Africa for a sub-Saharan African language. They governed through institutions sophisticated enough to persist for a millennium.

This history does not solve Sudan's present crisis. The knowledge that Amanirenas buried the head of Augustus beneath her victory temple does not feed a displaced family in Port Sudan or rebuild a school in Kordofan. But it changes the terms of the conversation. It replaces the question "how do we develop Sudan?" with a different question: "how do we rebuild what Sudan built before, and avoid the mistakes that caused it to be lost?"

That is a harder question. It is also a more honest one.
