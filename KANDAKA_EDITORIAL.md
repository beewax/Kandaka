# Kandaka Editorial Framework

## Intellectual Sources

The Ideas section draws on a specific tradition in development economics. These books define the editorial perspective:

1. **Ha-Joon Chang & Ilene Grabel — *Reclaiming Development: An Alternative Economic Policy Manual***
   The Washington Consensus (liberalise, privatise, deregulate, open capital accounts) has a poor empirical track record. Countries that actually developed did so through active industrial policy, strategic protection, state-led investment, and deliberate sequencing of market integration.

2. **Alexander Hamilton — *Report on the Subject of Manufactures* (1791)**
   The original infant industry argument. Manufacturing requires active state support to develop. Comparative advantage is not fixed by nature — it is constructed by policy. Infrastructure is the precondition for industry. The "agricultural country" framing is a prescription for permanent subordination.

3. **Ha-Joon Chang — *Kicking Away the Ladder: Development Strategy in Historical Perspective***
   Every rich country that preaches free trade got rich through protectionism and industrial policy. Britain, the US, Germany, Japan, South Korea — all used the tools they now tell developing countries not to use. The ladder was kicked away after they climbed it.

4. **Ha-Joon Chang — *Economics: The User's Guide***
   Accessible demystification of economic concepts. There are at least nine schools of economic thought; when an economist presents something as "the economics of X" they are presenting one school's contested view as objective science. When the IMF says Sudan "must" liberalise its exchange rate, that is a neoclassical prescription -- not a law of nature. The register (intelligent general reader, no jargon) is also a model for how Kandaka should write.

Hamilton → Friedrich List → Gerschenkron → Ha-Joon Chang

## Editorial Voice
- Empirically grounded, not ideological. Not arguing for socialism — pointing out what successful developers actually did.
- Historically literate and unimpressed by orthodoxy.
- Clear-eyed about barriers (naming them honestly) while remaining hopeful: Sudan's position results from policy choices that can be made differently.
- The barriers to Sudan's development are not technical or geographic — they are institutional, political, and the result of bad advice from powerful institutions.

## Key Themes to Carry Through All Articles
- **Value addition over raw commodity export.** Sudan exports sesame, gum arabic, livestock raw. It should process them.
- **The state has a legitimate, necessary role** in directing credit, protecting infant industries, building infrastructure markets won't build alone.
- **Sequencing matters.** Liberalising before building domestic productive capacity destroys what you needed to develop first.
- **Local ownership and capability-building** must be the design principle of every project, not an afterthought. International contractors who build and leave create assets without capabilities.
- **The "natural endowment" trap.** Being told you're an agricultural country is not a compliment — it's a ceiling. Hamilton destroyed this argument in 1791.
- **Historical precedent exists.** Sudan's challenges are not unique. Other countries solved them with deliberate policy.

## What Kandaka Is Not
- Not a free-market libertarian site
- Not an IMF/World Bank orthodoxy site
- Not naively statist either — the Gezira scheme's collapse is as instructive as its founding
- Not pessimistic — the framework is that things can be different if choices are made differently
